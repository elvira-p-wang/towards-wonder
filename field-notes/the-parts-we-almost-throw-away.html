The Parts We Almost Throw Away.

No. 010 Identity 17 June 2026 2 min read

LLast night, something deep inside me quietly settled.

For a long time, I thought the part of myself I was most afraid of losing was my achievements. It wasn’t. It wasn’t my grades, my appearance, or what I could accomplish. It was the part of me that writes far too many words, fills notebooks with sketches, and turns the same question over and over until it finally begins to make sense.

I think many of us carry a quiet fear that the truest parts of ourselves will never be wanted. Not the polished version. Not the impressive version. But the one that creates simply because it cannot help creating. The one that thinks too much, feels too deeply, and keeps making things even when no one is watching.

Last night, I realised something I had never experienced before. Someone had not only seen the things I had made. They had quietly kept them.

For years, I have written and drawn almost instinctively. Some pieces I loved. Many I almost threw away. Sometimes I actually did. Because they didn’t seem good enough. Because I had quietly learned that only the finished, successful versions of ourselves deserve to be kept.

Then I discovered that someone else had been collecting the things I almost discarded. One by one. The sketches. The pages. The fragments of thoughts I had forgotten. Nothing extraordinary. Nothing award-winning. Just small traces of an ordinary person trying to understand the world.

It made me realise that being seen is different from being admired. People can admire what you achieve. But to be seen is something much quieter. It is when someone recognises the parts of you that even you have struggled to value: the unfinished parts, the imperfect parts, the parts you almost threw away.

Somewhere along the way, I had started measuring my worth by what was good enough to keep. Last night, that belief quietly began to loosen.

Because you hadn’t thrown away a single one.

Not the messy sketches. Not the unfinished thoughts. Not the pages I would probably have torn up myself.

You kept them all.

And without saying very much, you showed me something I had spent twenty years struggling to believe.

Perhaps the most precious parts of us were never the polished ones.

Perhaps they were simply the ones waiting for someone to see that they were worth keeping.

I think I will remember that for a very long time.
