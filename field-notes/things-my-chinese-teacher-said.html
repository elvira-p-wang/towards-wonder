Things My Chinese Teacher Said.

No. 001 Education 11 July 2021 2 min read

II found myself reading through the notes I'd kept from my Chinese teacher.

Not because I wanted exam tips.

Because I wanted to remember the person behind them.

Some of the things she said stayed with me far longer than anything that appeared on a test paper.

"Not everyone has a bright future."

"The only thing I was good at in high school was admitting I wasn't."

"Some people avoid trying because it gives them an excuse. I could have done better if I'd tried. The truth is, they're afraid to discover what happens if they give everything they have and it's still not enough."

"The people who are truly working hard never complain about working hard."

"You spend all your time complaining that Classical Chinese is complicated, that it's pointless, that it's awful. But when you can't even understand these texts in the first place, you have neither the right nor the privilege to grow tired of them."

"Losing the ability to appreciate beauty is a tragedy."

"At the very least, as Chinese people."

"Know yourself. Be kind to yourself."

"Don't prepare your escape before the challenge has even begun."

"When I arrived at Peking University, everyone else went to registration. I went to the Temple of Earth. Later I realised that even the places I longed for weren't as perfect as I'd imagined."

"My dream is to stay a child forever. I spent years trying not to grow up. One day I realised that the moment you notice you don't want to grow up... you've already grown."

"When I was doing military training at Peking University, I buried a chair somewhere on campus. If you ever get into PKU, call me. I'll tell you where it is."

"I became a teacher because I wanted to find that feeling again. Thank you for letting me be a child again in Classroom 14."

"At first, I taught because I needed the money. I'm not noble. But somewhere along the way, I started caring about you. I love every one of you."

On the last day, she left us with one final sentence.

"No matter what happens later, you'll always be the best class I've ever taught."

Looking back, I realise she wasn't teaching us Chinese.

She was quietly teaching us how to become people.
